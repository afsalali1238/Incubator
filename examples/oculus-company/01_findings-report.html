Panel 01

The Thesis

For hardcore gamers and developers who have dreamed of true virtual reality, Oculus is a hardware startup that delivers a wide-field-of-view VR headset for under $300. Unlike military/enterprise simulators, it leverages cheap, high-resolution smartphone screens and sensors to make VR accessible to consumers.

Panel 02

The Market

The consumer VR market currently does not exist. The TAM is zero. We are making a bet that there is a latent market of PC gamers who will adopt the hardware if the price is right and the motion sickness is solved.

Panel 03

The Winners

No direct consumer VR analogues exist. Indirect successes include early PC hardware peripherals (like the first 3D graphics cards or the Nintendo Power Glove, though the latter failed on functionality).

Panel 05

The Graveyard

Nintendo Virtual Boy: Killed by red-tinted monochrome graphics, terrible ergonomics, and lack of true head tracking resulting in nausea. Virtuality (90s arcades): Killed by extreme cost and massive latency.

Panel 08

The Org

We cannot hire traditional software devs yet. We need deep-tech hardware hackers and an Optical/Sensor Engineer. The only thing that matters right now is bringing motion-to-photon latency under 20ms to prevent the "sim sickness" that killed 90s VR.

Panel 09

The Call

Verdict: BUILD (WITH CAUTION).

The hardware risk is extreme. If we cannot solve the latency, the project is dead. We will not spend a dime on marketing until a hardcore game developer (like John Carmack) puts it on and says "this doesn't make me sick."
