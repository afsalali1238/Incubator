Panel 01

The Thesis

For casual strategy players who find deep simulation games overwhelming, Terrarium Planet is a god-sim game that limits interactions to simple elemental placements (rain, heat, rock, seed). Unlike traditional 4X or management sims, it relies entirely on emergent cellular automata to create complex ecosystems from simple inputs. The riskiest assumption is that limiting player agency to "blunt tools" will feel satisfying rather than frustrating.

Panel 02

The Market

The "cozy game" and "casual sim" market is expanding, driven by players seeking low-stress, ambient experiences. TAM is broad (anyone who plays Stardew Valley or Animal Crossing), but the SOM is specific: players who want a "desktop pet" style ecosystem simulation that runs quietly in the background.

Panel 03

The Winners

We found 3 analogous successes:

  • WorldBox: Nailed the "god-game" scale by keeping graphics pixel-art and interactions physics-based.
  • The Powder Toy: Proved that pure cellular automata (sand falling, water flowing) is intrinsically fun even without a "game" layer.
  • Townscaper: Proved that removing "goals" and just letting players build beautiful things is a viable, highly monetizable wedge.
Panel 04

The Playbook

How the winners sequenced their builds:

  1. The Sandbox First: Built the physics engine (the sand, the water, the grid) before adding ANY characters or goals.
  2. The Visuals: Polished the "feel" (how the blocks snap or flow) to make the basic interaction inherently satisfying.
  3. The Viral Engine: Added a way to easily share "creations" or "worlds" (gifs, save codes) before doing any traditional marketing.

Panel 06

The Heresy (Devil's Advocate)

The strongest case against this project: "It's a toy, not a game." Without explicit goals or failure states, players might drop off after 10 minutes once they've seen all the elemental interactions. If the cellular automata isn't deep enough to surprise the player on day 3, the retention curve will flatline immediately.

Panel 07

The Trends

  • Tailwind: WebGL/WebGPU enables complex cellular automata directly in the browser, lowering friction to zero.
  • Tailwind: Rise of "second monitor games" — ambient experiences played alongside work.
  • Headwind: Infinite content generators (AI) make purely procedural visual toys less novel.
Panel 08

The Org

To build this, we need:

  1. Systems Designer: To design the elemental matrix and prevent simulation collapse.
  2. Frontend/WebGL Engineer: To make the cellular automata visually performant in the browser.
  3. Game Economist / Balancer: To pace the progression so players don't see everything in 10 minutes.
Panel 09

The Call

Verdict: BUILD.

The technical risk is low (cellular automata is well understood), but the design risk is high. The wedge is building a 4-element web prototype that is inherently fun to touch. We will test the "toy vs game" heresy by seeing if players spontaneously set their own goals.
