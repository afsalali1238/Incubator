01
The Thesis

Travelers priced out.
Homeowners with spare rooms.
One night changes everything.

For conference-goers and event travelers priced out of sold-out hotels, Airbnb is a peer-to-peer short-stay marketplace that lets them book a real home by the night. Unlike hotels (full) or VRBO/HomeAway (7-night minimums), Airbnb offers flexible, affordable, human stays — starting with one conference, one city, three hosts.

Riskiest Assumption

Homeowners will let strangers sleep in their homes. If false, the supply side evaporates and there is no marketplace. Cheap test: 3 hosts, 1 conference (IDSA SF, August 2008), sell out all 3. If hosts repeat → thesis holds.

02
The Market

A $500B hotel industry
with a structural gap at the edges.

Global hotel market ~$500B (2008). TAM is anyone who travels and sleeps somewhere. SAM is event-driven demand overflow (conferences, concerts, sports) where hotels sell out — estimated 15–20% of all hotel nights. SOM: SF event market in year 1.

Market shape
Oligopoly with a long tail

Marriott/Hilton/Hyatt own 40%+ of top-city capacity. Independent hosts own the long tail — millions of spare rooms priced at zero. The gap is there. Nobody is bridging it.

Timing signal
Trust infrastructure just arrived

Craigslist proved strangers transact. Facebook (2008: 100M users) normalized digital identity. PayPal is universal. The trust rails exist for the first time.

Who holds budget
Corporate travel + conference-goers

Conference attendees expense travel. Budget is real and annual. Repeat demand is built into the event calendar — not one-off.

Demand type
Expanding

Remote work, digital nomads, and event tourism are all growing. The addressable market expands every year as more people travel more flexibly.

Who solved the
cold-start problem and how.

04
The Playbook

The sequence that wins.
Not the features — the order.

Step 1 — Find the demand spike
Sold-out event, real city

Don't launch into thin air. Find where hotels are sold out and travelers are desperate. IDSA conference, SF, August 2008. This is your launch window.

Step 2 — Do it manually
Founders do the photography

eBay listed manually. Etsy went to craft fairs. You visit each host, shoot the photos, write the copy. Build host identity before you build host tools.

Step 3 — Nail trust before scale
Review + escrow from day one

Mutual review after every stay. Payment in escrow before check-in, released after. Not optional. Trust infrastructure is the entire product in year one.

Step 4 — Prove repeatability
Host says "I'd do it again"

The only metric that matters after IDSA. If your first 3 hosts repeat without founder handholding, the model works. Only then build self-serve tools.

Step 5 — City-by-city
Next event, next city

DNC (Denver, 2008) is your second launch. Same playbook: find demand spike, recruit hosts manually, go live 3 weeks before. Repeat until self-serve listing works.

Step 6 — PMF gate before scaling
"Very disappointed" >40%

Sean Ellis test at week 10. If >40% would be very disappointed without Airbnb, hire growth. If not, fix the product. Do not hire growth on a broken loop.

05
The Graveyard

Who died here.
And exactly why.

CompanyWedgeCause of DeathLesson
Wimdu (2011) Airbnb clone, Rocket Internet — $90M raised, launched in 80 cities in 3 months Bought supply without earning trust. Listings were low quality, hosts unmotivated. Guest experience collapsed. Acquired at distressed value 2018. You cannot buy host community. Quality gates and identity are the product, not the inventory count.
9flats (2011) European Airbnb clone, VC-backed No differentiated trust layer. Tried to win on price. Died when Airbnb expanded to Europe with genuine host relationships. Price is not a moat. Trust is.
Crashpadder (2010) UK short-stay marketplace, earlier than Airbnb in Europe Failed to build mutual-review loop early enough. Guests reported quality inconsistency. Acquired by Airbnb 2012 for the host base, not the product. First-mover advantage means nothing without review infrastructure. Build reviews before you build anything else.
Couchsurfing (monetization attempt) 35M+ users, deep trust community Tried to introduce payment after building a free community. Existing hosts rejected monetization as a betrayal of community values. Identity collapsed. Free-to-paid transitions destroy community trust. Start with money from day one.
06
The Heresy

The strongest case
this fails.

Here is the unflinching devil's advocate. If you can't beat these arguments, don't build.

🔴 The liability argument

One serious incident — theft, assault, property damage — goes viral and the supply side evaporates overnight. Insurance doesn't solve perception. The hotel industry will use every incident as PR. One bad story can kill the company before it reaches scale.

🔴 The regulation argument

Cities will regulate short-term rentals the moment they become popular. New York, San Francisco, and Paris have already moved against vacation rentals. If the regulatory environment turns hostile before you reach scale, you lose.

🔴 The hotel retaliation argument

Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt have lobbying budgets that dwarf your entire raise. They will back local regulation, pursue zoning enforcement, and run PR campaigns against home-sharing. They've beaten Uber-style disruption before.

🟡 Counter-position

All three risks are real and permanent. The answer is not to dismiss them — it's to move fast enough that by the time they materialize, you have sufficient supply density, host community loyalty, and political capital to fight back. The window is 18–24 months. Use it.

07
The Trends

What the next
24 months looks like.

Tailwind #1
Smartphone penetration accelerating

iPhone launched 2007. Android 2008. Mobile-first booking will be the default within 24 months. Airbnb built as mobile-first has structural advantage over hotels with legacy booking systems.

Tailwind #2
Financial crisis (2008)
People need extra income

The 2008 financial crisis is hitting in real time. Homeowners under mortgage pressure will rent spare rooms. Travelers will look for cheaper alternatives to hotels. Both sides of the marketplace are being pushed toward you.

Tailwind #3
Social trust infrastructure maturing

Facebook profiles, LinkedIn history, and Craigslist reputation are normalizing digital identity. The trust infrastructure required for peer-to-peer lodging is arriving at exactly the right moment.

Headwind
Short-term rental regulation risk

NYC and SF are already discussing short-term rental restrictions. This is a 3–5 year risk, not immediate — but regulatory strategy needs to start now, not after you're big enough to be a target.

09
The Call

CEO verdict:
BUILD.

The riskiest assumption is human trust, not technology. But the trust infrastructure — digital identity, payment rails, community norms from Craigslist and Couchsurfing — has just arrived. The 2008 financial crisis is pushing both sides of the marketplace toward you. The window is real and it is narrow.

The graveyard is full of companies that either moved without earning trust (Wimdu) or earned trust without monetizing it (Couchsurfing). The playbook is to start with money from day one, earn trust through manual host relationships before building tools, and hit 3 cities before the regulatory environment turns.

Build this
Peer-to-peer short-stay marketplace

SF only. Event-overflow demand only. Spare rooms only. Mutual review from booking #1. PayPal escrow manual. Expand only after IDSA validation.

Don't build this
Not a hotel aggregator. Not VRBO.

Not long-term rental. Not professional property managers. Not a search engine for existing listings. The scope fence is the product.

Cheap test — run in the next 3 weeks

3 SF hosts. IDSA Design Conference. Sell out all 3 nights. If 2 of 3 hosts say they'd do it again and 1 guest books a second stay — the thesis is validated. Total cost: your time and $0 in paid marketing.
