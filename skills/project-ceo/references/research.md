# Autonomous Research Method

This phase is fully autonomous. Once started, do not check in until the report is ready.
8–20 web_search calls is normal. Search each competitor and each question separately —
combined queries return shallow results. Believe surprising-but-sourced results; stay skeptical
of SEO-heavy listicles — corroborate.

---

## Probe 1 — Competitive landscape, tiered

Map competitors into four tiers. Search each named player individually.

- **Direct** — solving the same problem the same way.
- **Indirect** — solving the same problem a different way. Often the *real* competition:
  the spreadsheet, the manual workaround, the incumbent tool.
- **Potential acquirers** — bigger players who'd buy this rather than build it.
- **Adjacent** — players one step away who could move in.

For each tier, make the *strongest* case for why it threatens this project — not the
easy-to-dismiss version. Competitor neglect (underweighting what others do because you're
in love with your own vision) is the failure mode. The best founders know their enemies well.

---

## Probe 2 — The winners (the core deliverable)

Find **2–4 genuinely successful analogous projects**. They don't have to be direct competitors
— analogues count (a successful sandbox game is an analogue for a new god-sim even if the
theme differs). For each, reverse-engineer:

- **The wedge** — the narrow first thing they nailed before expanding. What was the initial
  hair-on-fire use case they locked in first?
- **The first version** — what their MVP actually was. Not what they say it was in retrospect
  — what it demonstrably was from early reviews, app store listings, or launch posts.
- **The build sequence** — what they shipped in what order. *This is the most valuable output
  of Phase 2.* The user wants to copy sequencing, not features.
- **Monetization** — how and when they started charging. Free → paid conversion, pricing
  model, what triggered willingness to pay.
- **The moat** — what's hard to replicate now. Data flywheel, workflow lock-in, community,
  regulatory approval, distribution, domain depth.

Carry the build sequences forward to Panel 04 of the report (The Playbook). That panel is
built almost entirely from this probe.

---

## Probe 3 — The graveyard (treat this with the same depth as the winners)

Find analogues or competitors that **died or stalled**. Search for shutdowns, pivots, and
"lessons learned" postmortems in this space. For each dead project, extract:

- **Their wedge** — what did they originally try to solve?
- **Their first version** — what did they actually ship?
- **When they started dying** — was it at launch, after initial traction, at scaling?
- **The cause of death** — be precise:
  - Wrong market (nobody cared enough, workaround was good enough)
  - Premature scaling (hired too fast before product-market fit)
  - No moat (got cloned by a better-resourced player)
  - Mistiming (right idea, wrong moment — regulation, tech, or behavior wasn't ready)
  - Feature bloat (built everything, nailed nothing)
  - Founder issues (team collapse, burnout, co-founder split)
  - Unit economics (CAC > LTV, couldn't make the math work)
- **The lesson for this project** — what specific risk does this graveyard entry reveal?

Present as a cause-of-death table in Panel 05 with a row per dead project and a "lesson"
column. This is where founders stop looking. It's the most underrated section.

---

## Probe 4 — Market shape

Sketch TAM/SAM/SOM from public data. Don't manufacture a fundable-looking number — pressure-
test the assumptions behind it.

- Is the market **expanding** (new behavior, new tech, new regulation creating demand),
  **consolidating** (a few players winning, barriers rising), or **mature** (stable, requires
  displacement)?
- Who holds budget vs who influences the decision? (In B2B: the buyer ≠ the user ≠ the
  economic decision-maker. Map all three.)
- What's the cost of the status quo? If the current workaround is "free" (a spreadsheet,
  a manual process), willingness to pay needs a reason.

---

## Probe 5 — Trends

Identify **2–3 external trends** that will move this market in ~24 months. Label each:

- **Tailwind** — accelerates this project's value proposition
- **Headwind** — makes this project harder to win

Trend categories: regulatory (new law, enforcement change), technological (cost collapse of
a key input, new capability), demographic (behavior shift in the target population),
economic (recession/growth changes willingness to spend).

---

## Probe 6 — Devil's advocate (mandatory, never skip)

Make the strongest possible case that a competitor wins and this project doesn't:
- Why their approach is better suited to the market
- Why customers choose them over this
- Why the key differentiator isn't as defensible as it appears
- Why the timing is wrong (too early, too late, wrong moment)

If the research genuinely surfaces evidence the idea needs revision — a key assumption broken,
a competitor already doing this better — **the report must say so**. Put it in Panel 06 (The
Heresy) and flag it in Panel 09 (The Call). Don't manufacture a fundable-looking narrative to
please the founder. A research engine pointed at confirming a bad idea is worse than no research.

---

## Discipline

- Cite real sources. Paraphrase findings — never reproduce source text. One short quote per
  source maximum, under 15 words.
- When done with all probes, write the report (Phase 3). Do not ask permission.
- Carry forward to the report: the tiered competitor map, 2–4 winners with build sequences,
  graveyard table with causes of death and lessons, market shape, trends, and your synthesized
  devil's-advocate case.
